# LinkedIn Organic Content Strategy for B2B SaaS
## A Practitioner Playbook

**Author:** Afreen Irfan  
**Topic:** LinkedIn Organic Content Strategy for B2B SaaS  
**Based on research from:** Ivan Dimitrijevic, Richard van der Blom, Matt Barker, Michelle J Raymond, Tommy Clark, Ben B2B, HubSpot Marketing (Ross Simmonds), Exit Five (Dave Gerhardt), SaaS Talk (Metrics Brothers), Marketing Against the Grain (Kipp Bodnar & Kieran Flanagan)

## Why This Playbook Exists

Most LinkedIn advice is written for personal brand builders, solopreneurs, or coaches. This playbook is written specifically for B2B SaaS companies — early to mid-stage — who want to use LinkedIn organic content to build pipeline, not just followers.

It is built from primary research: LinkedIn posts, YouTube transcripts, and podcast episodes from 10 practitioners who either work with B2B SaaS companies directly or have built B2B SaaS companies themselves using LinkedIn as a growth channel.

Every recommendation below cites its source. Where experts disagree, I say so — and I take a side.

## Part 1: The Foundation — Before You Post Anything

### 1.1 Understand What LinkedIn Actually Is in 2026

LinkedIn is not a posting channel. It is a trust engine.

By the time a B2B buyer reaches out to a SaaS company, they have already moved through six invisible stages: exposure, familiarity, memory, trust, internal preference, and shortlist. Most CRMs only see the deal at the very end. The buying decision is shaped long before a lead appears in any dashboard.

*(Source: Ivan Dimitrijevic, LinkedIn post, 23.04.2026, linkedin.com/in/ivan-dimitrijevic)*

This means LinkedIn content must work at the top of the funnel — building trust and credibility before anyone is ready to buy. Companies that only post when they want to generate leads are already too late.

### 1.2 Define Your ICP Before Writing a Single Post

Before posting anything, answer one question clearly:

**"Why should someone in my ICP follow this account?"**

If you cannot answer this in one sentence, do not start posting. The most common failure for B2B SaaS companies on LinkedIn is posting product updates and company news without giving their ideal customer profile (ICP) a clear reason to follow.

*(Source: Tommy Clark, "The Best LinkedIn Content Strategy for Early-Stage SaaS," YouTube, 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@ptclarksmedia)*

Your ICP answer should be specific. Not "we help companies hire better" but "we post weekly data and frameworks on how startup founders build their first 10-person team." The narrower and more specific, the better.

### 1.3 Understand the New Algorithm Before You Build Strategy

LinkedIn's algorithm in 2026 no longer evaluates posts in isolation. It evaluates your entire professional identity across three integrated signals: your profile, your posts, and your engagement — essentially your positioning.

This means:
- Posting frequently without clear added value now actively hurts your reach
- A well-written post that is misaligned with your profile identity will not travel far
- Niche relevance outperforms volume, every time

*(Source: Richard van der Blom, LinkedIn post, 23.04.2026, linkedin.com/in/richardvanderblom)*

**Practical implication:** Before optimising your content calendar, optimise your LinkedIn profile. Your headline, banner, and about section must clearly signal who you help and how. A misaligned profile kills even great content.

## Part 2: Account Structure — Who Should Post

### 2.1 Start With the Founder's Personal Account

Personal accounts consistently and significantly outperform company pages on LinkedIn. This is not an opinion — it is backed by data:

- Shopify's president Harley got double the engagement of the official Shopify brand page
- Lavender's co-founder got 130+ likes on a personal post vs 19 likes on the same content from the company account
- SparkToro founder Rand Fishkin's video got 1,000+ likes vs 20 on the brand post

*(Source: Tommy Clark, "The Best LinkedIn Content Strategy for Early-Stage SaaS," YouTube, 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@ptclarksmedia)*

For early-stage B2B SaaS, this means the founder or CEO posting from their personal account is the highest-ROI LinkedIn activity available. Do this before anything else.

### 2.2 Build the Content Ecosystem in Layers

At scale, a B2B SaaS LinkedIn presence should have three layers working together:

1. **Founder-led content** — highest ROI, start here immediately
2. **Company page content** — builds brand credibility and searchability
3. **Employee-led content** — amplifies reach and adds human diversity of voice

Beehiiv is cited as the best real-world example of this ecosystem in action: founder Tyler Denk posts regularly, the company page is active, and employees amplify organically.

*(Source: Tommy Clark, "The Best LinkedIn Content Strategy for Early-Stage SaaS," YouTube, 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@ptclarksmedia)*

### 2.3 Treat the Company Page as a Conversion Asset

The company page is not just a brand presence — it is a conversion surface. Visitors arrive through search, through content shares, and through employee profiles. The page must convert them into followers, newsletter subscribers, or demo bookings.

Most companies are getting their company pages wrong. The five most commonly missed strategies for company page conversion are: a clear value proposition in the header, consistent visual branding, strategic use of the Featured section, regular native content, and employee profile alignment.

*(Source: Michelle J Raymond, LinkedIn post and SMMS presentation "LinkedIn Company Pages That Convert: The 5 Strategies Everyone Else Misses," 23.04.2026, linkedin.com/in/michellejraymond)*

**The Power of Two principle:** Employee profiles and company pages reflect on each other. When you tag a colleague in a company post, their profile quality directly affects your post's credibility. A poor profile on a tagged employee hurts performance. Invest in the whole team's LinkedIn presence, not just the company page.

*(Source: Michelle J Raymond, LinkedIn post, 23.04.2026, linkedin.com/in/michellejraymond)*

## Part 3: Content Strategy — What to Post

### 3.1 Choose Value-Based Content Over Entertainment-Based Content

For B2B SaaS, content should map directly to the problem your product solves. Entertainment-based content builds bigger audiences but attracts the wrong people. The goal is not followers — it is pipeline.

Use the three-part Venn diagram as your content filter. Every post should sit at the intersection of:
- Relevance to your product
- Value to your ICP
- Founder or brand credibility

If a post idea does not hit all three, do not post it.

*(Source: Tommy Clark, "The Best LinkedIn Content Strategy for Early-Stage SaaS," YouTube, 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@ptclarksmedia)*

### 3.2 Use the Four E Content Framework

Map your content calendar to four content types that balance trust-building with engagement:

- **Educate** — teach your ICP something directly related to what you sell
- **Engage** — create two-way conversations, ask questions, run polls
- **Entertain** — relatable content, memes, behind-the-scenes moments
- **Empower** — celebrate client wins, share results and success screenshots

*(Source: Ross Simmonds, HubSpot Marketing YouTube channel, "The Latest LinkedIn Lead Generation Strategies That Generated $2M+," 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@HubSpotMarketing)*

Rotate through all four types weekly. Most B2B SaaS companies only Educate — which builds authority but not community. Add Empower and Engage posts to deepen audience relationships.

### 3.3 Build 3-5 Content Pillars

Identify the themes you want your brand associated with. Stick to 3-5 pillars maximum. Posting about everything means owning nothing.

Example pillars for a B2B SaaS hiring platform:
- AI in recruitment (trend-led)
- Founder hiring stories (narrative)
- Hiring metrics and benchmarks (data-led)
- Team building mistakes (contrarian)
- Customer success stories (proof)

Every post you write should belong to one of these pillars. If it does not fit, cut it.

*(Source: Ross Simmonds, HubSpot Marketing YouTube channel, 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@HubSpotMarketing)*

### 3.4 Use the 5 Post Types in Weekly Rotation

A proven weekly content calendar for B2B SaaS (5-7 posts per week):

**Type 1 — Personal business story**
A personal anecdote with a business takeaway tied to your product or ICP. Opens with a human moment, ends with a professional insight.

**Type 2 — Story-based listicle**
Starts with a hook story, then organises takeaways as a numbered list. Creates curiosity and is easy to write at scale.

**Type 3 — Customer anecdote**
The "I was on a call with a customer" format. Takes a real conversation and turns it into a post with a business insight. High trust, high relatability.

**Type 4 — Polarising industry opinion**
A strong stance on something your ICP cares about. Drives comments. Only share opinions you genuinely believe. Manufactured controversy reads as fake.

**Type 5 — Case study post**
Social proof, formatted for native LinkedIn reading. Structure: hook → context → problem → solution → impact → CTA. No external links. Once per week.

*(Source: Tommy Clark, "The Best LinkedIn Content Strategy for Early-Stage SaaS," YouTube, 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@ptclarksmedia)*

### 3.5 Use Impressions Arbitrage — Connect Trends to Your Core Message

Identify topics that are top of mind for your audience right now and tie them directly back to your product or positioning. This is impressions arbitrage: borrowing the reach of a trending conversation and redirecting it toward your core message.

Example: A B2B SaaS company in the hiring space should not just post about hiring. They should post about AI in hiring, because that topic generates significantly higher organic reach while staying on-brand.

Data point: Posts about AI writing got 4-5x more impressions than average posts for a LinkedIn writing practitioner — without changing the core message.

*(Source: Matt Barker, LinkedIn post, 23.04.2026, linkedin.com/in/mattbarker93)*

## Part 4: Writing — How to Write Posts That Perform

### 4.1 The Hook Is Everything

If the first line does not stop someone from scrolling, nothing else matters. On LinkedIn, users see only the first 1-2 lines before the "see more" button. The entire goal of the hook is to get that click.

A strong hook:
- Stops the scroll with curiosity or a bold claim
- Uses specific numbers where possible
- Signals relevance to the exact job title of your ICP
- Never starts with "I" or "We"

The goal of every post is to earn the "see more" click. Everything else comes after.

*(Source: Ross Simmonds, HubSpot Marketing YouTube channel, 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@HubSpotMarketing)*

### 4.2 Structure Every Post in Three Parts

Every LinkedIn post has three components:

- **Hook** — spend the most time here. One to two lines maximum.
- **Body** — let it flow. Use listicle format for easy reading. Short paragraphs.
- **CTA** — be direct, not desperate. Tell people clearly what to do next without sounding salesy.

*(Source: Tommy Clark, "The Best LinkedIn Content Strategy for Early-Stage SaaS," YouTube, 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@ptclarksmedia)*

### 4.3 AI Tools Support Writing — They Do Not Replace Judgment

AI tools like Claude can help format and structure LinkedIn posts — but only if the writer already understands what good LinkedIn writing looks like. Giving a lazy prompt to an AI and posting the output will produce generic, average content.

The correct workflow: write a rough draft with your real voice and real insight, then use AI to tighten structure, improve flow, or generate alternative hooks. Never use AI as the source of ideas or opinions.

*(Source: Matt Barker, LinkedIn post, 23.04.2026, linkedin.com/in/mattbarker93)*

## Part 5: Distribution and Timing

### 5.1 Post Frequency — Consistency Beats Volume

Post 5-7 times per week — approximately once per day on weekdays. Do not post more than once per day as this suppresses reach on LinkedIn. Posting once or twice per month is not enough to build an audience.

*(Source: Tommy Clark, "The Best LinkedIn Content Strategy for Early-Stage SaaS," YouTube, 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@ptclarksmedia)*

Dave Gerhardt's personal workflow is the most disciplined template available: post Monday to Friday, once per idea per day, never duplicate content in the same day, use a content bank of queued ideas.

*(Source: Dave Gerhardt, Exit Five Podcast Episodes #275 and #139, exitfive.com)*

### 5.2 Understand Post Lifespan — Not All Posts Die the Same Way

Based on analysis of 1.3 million LinkedIn posts, there are four distinct post lifespan patterns:

**The Rocket (45% of posts):** 40% of reach lands within 4 hours, 70% within day one. Done by day 5. Nurture by replying to every comment within the first 4 hours.

**The Builder (30% of posts):** Modest start, steady climb through day 8. Save rates are high. Nurture by re-entering the thread on days 2-3 with a fresh insight.

**The Grower (20% of posts):** Only 30% reach on day one, then pulses across two weeks. Do not abandon after 48 hours — add a valuable comment on day 3-4 to trigger redistribution.

**The Afterburner (5% of posts):** Underperforms days 1-2, then ignites on day 3-4. Double down when it sparks — reply aggressively and share it yourself to accelerate the second wave.

*(Source: Richard van der Blom, LinkedIn post and Algorithm Insights Report 2026, 23.04.2026, linkedin.com/in/richardvanderblom)*

**Practical implication:** Do not judge a post's performance in the first 48 hours. Some of your best content takes time to find its audience.

### 5.3 Early Engagement Is a Signal

Get your team to engage with posts in the first 0-30 minutes after publishing. Early engagement signals to the LinkedIn algorithm that the content is worth distributing more widely. This is not gaming the algorithm — it is giving your content a fair start.

*(Source: Tommy Clark, "The Best LinkedIn Content Strategy for Early-Stage SaaS," YouTube, 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@ptclarksmedia)*

### 5.4 Turn Organic Content Into Paid Amplification

Once you have posts performing organically, boost them using LinkedIn Thought Leader Ads. This allows brands to promote posts from individual employees or founders as ads — adding paid reach without losing the human, authentic feel of organic content.

*(Source: Dave Gerhardt, Exit Five Podcast Episode #317, exitfive.com)*

## Part 6: The Content Flywheel — LinkedIn Is Not the End

A successful LinkedIn post is a signal, not the finish line. If a post performs well, that topic has validated audience interest. Use it:

LinkedIn post → Newsletter issue → Community discussion → Webinar theme → Podcast episode → Back to LinkedIn

This flywheel is how Exit Five built a 5,000-member private B2B marketing community with no paid acquisition. The LinkedIn content drives awareness; each subsequent layer deepens the relationship.

*(Source: Dave Gerhardt, Exit Five Podcast Episode #225, exitfive.com)*

**Practical rule:** When a post significantly outperforms your average, extract the core idea and turn it into at least one other content format within 7 days.

## Part 7: Measurement — What to Track and When

### 7.1 Two Buckets of Metrics

**Social metrics** (track weekly):
- Impressions per post
- Follower growth rate
- Engagement rate (comments weighted more than likes)
- Save rate (a strong signal of high-value content)

**Business metrics** (track monthly):
- Inbound leads self-reporting LinkedIn as their source
- Email list growth attributed to LinkedIn
- Self-reported attribution in sales calls: "How did you hear about us?"

*(Source: Tommy Clark, "The Best LinkedIn Content Strategy for Early-Stage SaaS," YouTube, 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@ptclarksmedia)*

### 7.2 Expect a 1-3 Month Lag Before Business Results

Do not measure LinkedIn content ROI in the first 30 days. Expect a 1-3 month lag before business metrics (inbound leads, self-reported attribution) start flowing from consistent LinkedIn activity. LinkedIn is a long game.

*(Source: Tommy Clark, "The Best LinkedIn Content Strategy for Early-Stage SaaS," YouTube, 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@ptclarksmedia)*

### 7.3 Measure Salience, Not Just Awareness

The goal of LinkedIn organic content is not brand awareness. It is salience — being the brand that comes to mind when a prospect faces a specific problem. The difference is critical.

Awareness means someone has heard of you. Salience means someone thinks of you at the exact moment they need you.

Measure salience through qualitative sales call data: "What made you reach out now?" and "Who else did you consider?" are more valuable questions than any vanity metric.

*(Source: Ben B2B, "11 Best B2B Marketing Strategies for 2026," YouTube, 23.04.2026, youtube.com/@benb2b)*

## Part 8: Where Experts Disagree

### Disagreement 1 — Personal Profile vs Company Page: Which Should Come First?

**Tommy Clark's position:** Always start with the founder's personal account. Personal accounts consistently get 3-5x more engagement than company pages. The data is unambiguous. For early-stage SaaS, the founder posting is the highest-ROI activity on LinkedIn.
*(Source: Tommy Clark, YouTube, 23.04.2026)*

**Michelle J Raymond's position:** Company pages are the underrated engine of B2B LinkedIn growth. Most companies neglect them, which is a strategic mistake. A well-built company page converts visitors, enables employee advocacy at scale, and builds brand credibility independently of whether the founder is posting.
*(Source: Michelle J Raymond, LinkedIn posts and SMMS presentation, 23.04.2026)*

**My take: Tommy is right for the first 12 months. Michelle is right for the next 3 years.**

For an early-stage B2B SaaS company with limited resources, founder-led content on a personal account will generate faster pipeline with less effort. The algorithm favours people over brands, and founder credibility transfers directly to the product. However, Michelle is not wrong — a company page that converts is a long-term asset that compounds over time. The correct sequencing is: personal account first, company page optimised in parallel, company page as a primary content channel later once the brand has established credibility. They are not either/or — they are a sequence.

---

### Disagreement 2 — Posting Frequency: Volume vs Niche Relevance

**Tommy Clark's position:** Post 5-7 times per week. Consistency and frequency are what builds an audience on LinkedIn. Posting once or twice per month is not enough.
*(Source: Tommy Clark, YouTube, 23.04.2026)*

**Richard van der Blom's position:** Posting frequently without clear added value now actively hurts your account. Niche relevance outperforms volume every time. The 2026 algorithm evaluates your entire professional identity — a misaligned post damages your profile's credibility signals.
*(Source: Richard van der Blom, LinkedIn post, 23.04.2026)*

**My take: Richard is asking the right question. Tommy is giving the right number.**

These two positions are not actually contradictory — they are addressing different failure modes. Richard is warning against posting junk at high volume. Tommy is warning against posting too rarely. The correct answer is: post 5-7 times per week, but only publish posts that are genuinely relevant to your ICP and aligned with your positioning. High frequency AND high relevance is the goal. Frequency without relevance is spam. Relevance without frequency is invisibility.

---

### Disagreement 3 — What Is the Primary Goal of LinkedIn Content?

**Ivan Dimitrijevic's position:** The goal of LinkedIn is to build trust and credibility — to earn the "invisible shortlist." Being visible is not enough. Buyability comes from signal quality: clear positioning, specific proof, sharp messaging, coherent team presence. Visibility fills the room; credibility moves the deal.
*(Source: Ivan Dimitrijevic, LinkedIn posts, 23.04.2026)*

**Ben B2B's position:** The goal of LinkedIn is salience — being the brand that comes to mind when a prospect faces a specific problem. Not brand awareness, not even trust. Salience. The 211-day average B2B buying journey means your content must create a memory trace long before anyone is ready to buy.
*(Source: Ben B2B, YouTube, 23.04.2026)*

**My take: These are the same idea described at different layers — and both are correct.**

Ivan is describing the mechanism (trust and credibility signals). Ben is describing the outcome (salience — being remembered). A brand that builds genuine credibility on LinkedIn will, as a result, achieve salience. The practical implication of both positions is identical: stop posting for immediate lead generation and start posting to earn the right to be chosen when the buyer is ready. The difference in framing is useful though — Ivan's framework guides what you post (credibility signals), and Ben's framework guides why you post (to own a moment in the buyer's mind).

## Part 9: What I Rejected and Why

### Rejected Idea 1 — Direct Mail as a LinkedIn Amplifier

Ben B2B recommends combining physical direct mail with LinkedIn engagement signals — identifying companies that engage with your LinkedIn content and sending them physical mail as a "super touch point."

**Why I rejected it:** This is a compelling idea for companies with established LinkedIn audiences and budget for physical mail campaigns. It is not appropriate advice for this playbook. A B2B SaaS company in early stage does not have the audience size to generate meaningful LinkedIn engagement signals, the operational infrastructure to run direct mail campaigns, or the budget to test it responsibly. Including it here would create the impression that LinkedIn organic strategy requires expensive multi-channel follow-up — which would discourage companies from starting. LinkedIn organic is valuable precisely because it costs time, not money. This idea belongs in a paid acquisition or enterprise ABM playbook, not here.

### Rejected Idea 2 — Posting on Bing Ads as a LinkedIn Content Complement

Ben B2B recommends shifting B2B ad spend to Bing Ads as an underused channel with cheaper traffic and higher conversion rates than Google Ads.

**Why I rejected it:** This is a paid media recommendation in a playbook specifically about organic LinkedIn content strategy. Mixing paid and organic strategies in the same playbook creates confusion about where to prioritise resources. Early-stage B2B SaaS companies reading this playbook are most likely choosing between spending money on ads or investing time in organic. Recommending Bing Ads here muddies that decision. The insight is not wrong — it is simply out of scope. Paid media strategy should live in a separate playbook.

## Part 10: My Original Ideas

### Original Idea — The "Content Alibi" System

None of the ten experts I researched mentioned this concept directly. I am calling it the Content Alibi System.

**The idea:** Every LinkedIn post a B2B SaaS company publishes should function as a sales alibi — a piece of content that gives your sales team a genuine, non-awkward reason to reach out to warm prospects.

Here is how it works in practice:

1. Publish a LinkedIn post on a topic your ICP cares about (e.g., "3 signs your hiring process is slowing down your growth")
2. Before publishing, identify 10-15 prospects from your CRM or LinkedIn search who fit this exact pain point
3. After publishing, your sales team reaches out with one line: "We just published something that made me think of you — thought it might be relevant to what you're building"
4. The content does the credibility work; the outreach creates the moment

**Why it could work:** Ross Simmonds (HubSpot channel) established that content builds trust so that outbound DMs convert better because the prospect already knows who you are. Dave Gerhardt (Exit Five) established that LinkedIn is a non-paid pipeline channel. Ivan Dimitrijevic established that the buyer journey runs through trust before it reaches a sales conversation.

The Content Alibi System connects all three insights into a repeatable workflow. It transforms every piece of organic content into a direct sales trigger without making the sales team sound cold or transactional. The content gives them permission to reach out; the insight gives the prospect a reason to reply.

**What makes it original:** Every expert I researched treats LinkedIn content and LinkedIn outreach as separate activities. The Content Alibi System treats every post as an outbound trigger — making the content calendar and the sales sequence one integrated system, not two separate ones.

## Part 11: Weaknesses of This Playbook

**1. It assumes a founder or senior team member is willing and able to post consistently.**
The entire strategy rests on founder-led or executive-led content as the primary engine. In practice, many founders either do not want to be the face of the brand, do not have time to post consistently, or are not comfortable writing publicly. This playbook does not address what to do when the founder is not the right person for LinkedIn. A ghostwriting strategy or employee advocacy programme would need to be built separately.

**2. The measurement framework is incomplete for early-stage companies.**
Self-reported attribution in sales calls only works when you are already having sales calls. Companies in the first 3-6 months of their LinkedIn strategy will have no business metrics to track yet. The 1-3 month lag warning helps, but does not solve the problem of how to know if the strategy is working before pipeline data exists.

**3. The posting frequency recommendation may not be realistic for a small team.**
Posting 5-7 times per week requires either a content bank built in advance or a significant weekly time investment. For a two-person marketing team running a product launch, onboarding, and multiple channels simultaneously, this cadence is likely to break. A realistic minimum viable cadence for under-resourced teams is not addressed here.

**4. This playbook is based on 2025-2026 LinkedIn conditions.**
Richard van der Blom explicitly noted that most LinkedIn advice in 2026 is already outdated because LinkedIn keeps changing the algorithm. The post lifespan data, engagement signals, and algorithm behaviour documented here are accurate as of April 2026 — but LinkedIn's algorithm updates frequently. This playbook should be treated as a live document and reviewed against Richard's annual Algorithm Insights Report when each new edition is published.

**5. The B2B buying journey data is not sourced from LinkedIn-specific research.**
The 211-day B2B buying journey statistic cited from Ben B2B comes from Dream Data's analysis of 23,000 sessions. This is compelling data, but it covers B2B journeys generally — not LinkedIn-specific journeys. The assumption that LinkedIn organic content maps neatly onto a 211-day buying cycle is a reasonable inference, not a proven causal link.

## Part 12: Who I Would NOT Recommend Following — and Why

**I would not recommend HubSpot Marketing's YouTube channel as a primary source for early-stage B2B SaaS LinkedIn strategy.**

This needs nuance, because the Ross Simmonds video published on that channel is genuinely excellent and is cited throughout this playbook. My concern is not with individual videos — it is with using HubSpot Marketing as a go-to LinkedIn strategy source.

Here is why:

**1. HubSpot is not a useful benchmark for most B2B SaaS companies.** HubSpot is a $2B+ revenue public company with hundreds of marketers, a globally recognised brand, and a decade of content marketing investment. When their content says "here is what works on LinkedIn," it is describing what works when you already have enormous brand authority, a massive email list to cross-promote with, and a budget most early-stage SaaS companies will never see. The advice is correct for HubSpot. It does not transfer.

**2. The channel optimises for reach, not specificity.** HubSpot's YouTube channel has 669K subscribers and covers everything from social media to AI to email marketing. The content is designed for the broadest possible B2B marketing audience. For someone trying to build a specific, executable LinkedIn strategy for an early-stage SaaS company, this is the wrong level of specificity. You will get good general frameworks and almost no tactical depth.

**3. There is a conflict of interest that is never disclosed.** HubSpot recommends using HubSpot tools throughout its content. This is understandable — it is their channel — but it means every framework and workflow they present is shaped around their own product ecosystem. A founder choosing tools and strategies based on HubSpot's content is implicitly being guided toward HubSpot's paid products. This is not dishonest, but it should be named.

I want to be clear: the Ross Simmonds video is an exception and is worth watching. But if you are looking for a consistent, trustworthy source of LinkedIn strategy specifically for B2B SaaS, follow Tommy Clark, Richard van der Blom, and Dave Gerhardt instead. They are more specific, more honest about limitations, and more relevant to where most B2B SaaS companies actually are.
